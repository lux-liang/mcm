\begin{abstract}
The accelerating deployment of Generative AI (Gen-AI) systems confronts labor economics with a fundamental paradox: technologies engineered for human augmentation may, beyond critical adoption thresholds, precipitate employment displacement. We term this the \textbf{Tipping Point Paradox} and develop the \textbf{\fullmodelname{} (\modelname{})} framework to elucidate its underlying mechanisms and identify conditions for its resolution.

Our theoretical contribution centers on three interconnected dynamical models. \textbf{Model I} (Task Decomposition) operationalizes occupation-level AI susceptibility through granular task analysis, yielding a Composite Exposure Index ($\Xi_o$) and Physical Protection Index ($P_i$) derived from O*NET work activity data. \textbf{Model II} (Verification-Bottleneck Congestion) formalizes the mechanism whereby finite human oversight capacity constrains sustainable AI adoption, generating occupation-specific tipping points ($A^* = 1/2s$) where employment dynamics transition from augmentation to displacement. \textbf{Model III} (Educational Feedback) demonstrates how curriculum interventions modulate workforce capability ($V_s$), thereby shifting tipping points rightward and extending the augmentation window.

Empirical application to three archetypal occupations---\textit{Information Security Analysts} (STEM), \textit{Electricians} (Trade), and \textit{Graphic Designers} (Arts)---reveals divergent trajectories. Electricians exhibit robust physical protection ($P_i = 0.78$) with adoption constrained below 10\%; Information Security Analysts occupy a permanent augmentation basin with tipping points exceeding feasible adoption levels; Graphic Designers approach the displacement threshold ($A^* = 90.9\%$ versus $A_{\text{cap}} = 85\%$), though educational intervention can avert this outcome. The Graphic Designer case exemplifies \textit{unit labor value dilution}: stable employment (+0.2\%) masks wage compression (-4\%) as productivity gains dissipate through market saturation rather than accruing to workers.

Validation against BLS employment data (2010--2022) yields baseline calibration MAPE below 7\% across occupations; we conduct post-2023 stress tests under explicitly stated GenAI scenarios, with scenario parameters subject to comprehensive sensitivity analysis. Institution-specific recommendations follow: Carnegie Mellon University (maintain enrollment; frontier AI integration), Lansing Community College (expand 9--13\%; auxiliary tools), Rhode Island School of Design (maintain enrollment; curriculum AI-intensity $\phi = 0.25$). The framework generalizes to any occupation amenable to task decomposition, offering a principled analytical apparatus for navigating the AI-employment nexus. We conclude that the tipping point is not destiny but a design parameter---amenable to institutional intervention, policy modulation, and proactive human capital investment.
\end{abstract}
