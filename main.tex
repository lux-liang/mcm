% ===== Summary Sheet =====
\begin{abstract}
The proliferation of Generative AI (Gen-AI) presents a paradox: technologies designed to augment human productivity may, beyond critical thresholds, trigger employment displacement. This paper develops the \textbf{\fullmodelname{} (\modelname{})} framework to resolve this paradox through task-level analysis of three archetypal occupations---\textit{Information Security Analysts} (STEM), \textit{Electricians} (Trade), and \textit{Graphic Designers} (Arts).

Our framework introduces three interconnected models: \textbf{Model I} (Task Decomposition \& Risk Assessment) decomposes occupations into weighted task units using O*NET data, quantifying AI susceptibility through a novel Composite Exposure Index. \textbf{Model II} (Verification-Bottleneck Congestion Dynamics) formalizes how human verification capacity constrains AI adoption, revealing occupation-specific ``tipping points'' where marginal AI benefits turn negative. \textbf{Model III} (Educational Feedback Loop) demonstrates how targeted curriculum interventions can shift these tipping points rightward, extending the ``augmentation window.''

Key findings include: (1) \textbf{Physical Protection Effect}---Electricians exhibit a robust ``protection period'' with adoption ceiling at 9.3\%, contrasting sharply with Graphic Designers' 85\% ceiling; (2) \textbf{Verification Congestion Paradox}---high AI adoption (>70\%) triggers congestion ratios exceeding 30\%, inverting productivity gains; (3) \textbf{Multi-Channel Learning}---workers acquire AI skills through job, social, and autonomous channels, with social learning contributing 40\% of capability growth for low-adoption occupations.

Based on simulation results validated against BLS data (2010--2024), we provide differentiated recommendations for Carnegie Mellon University (maintain enrollment, frontier AI integration), Lansing Community College (expand 10.8\%, auxiliary tool approach), and Rhode Island School of Design (maintain enrollment, creative-AI workflow redesign). The framework generalizes to any occupation decomposable into task units, offering a principled approach to navigating the AI-employment nexus.
\end{abstract}
