% ===== Summary Sheet =====
\begin{abstract}
The accelerating deployment of Generative AI (Gen-AI) confronts labor economics with a fundamental paradox: technologies engineered for augmentation may, beyond critical adoption thresholds, precipitate displacement. We develop the \textbf{\fullmodelname{} (\modelname{})} framework comprising three dynamical models: \textbf{Model I} (Task Decomposition) yields a Composite Exposure Index ($\Xi_o$) and Physical Protection Index ($P_i$) from O*NET data; \textbf{Model II} (Verification-Bottleneck Congestion) generates occupation-specific tipping points ($A^* = 1/2s$) where employment transitions from augmentation to displacement; \textbf{Model III} (Educational Feedback) demonstrates how curriculum interventions shift tipping points rightward.

Application to three archetypal occupations reveals divergent trajectories: Electricians exhibit robust physical protection ($P_i = 0.78$, adoption $<10\%$); Information Security Analysts occupy a permanent augmentation basin ($A^* > 150\%$); Graphic Designers approach the displacement threshold ($A^* = 90.9\%$ vs.\ $A_{\text{cap}} = 85\%$), though educational intervention averts this outcome. Calibration against BLS data (2010--2022) yields MAPE $<7\%$; GenAI-specific parameters are treated as scenario assumptions subject to sensitivity analysis. Institution-specific recommendations: Carnegie Mellon (maintain enrollment; frontier AI), Lansing CC (expand 9--13\%), RISD (curriculum AI-intensity $\phi = 0.25$). We conclude that the tipping point is not destiny but a design parameter.
\end{abstract}
